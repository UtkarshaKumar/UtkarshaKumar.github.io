How Decentralized Compute Co-ops Could Replace SaaS in the Age of AI Agents

· Originally published on Notion ↗

The era of paying monthly fees for AI intelligence is going to end. But the compute bill? That’s just beginning.

The $85,000 Problem

In 2025, the average organization spent $85,521 per month on AI infrastructure — a 36% jump from the previous year. For enterprises deploying autonomous agents across sales, support, and operations, monthly costs routinely exceed $50,000. One customer service AI handling 500,000 conversations monthly can burn through $5,000-$25,000 in API tokens alone.

The economics have flipped. As AI models approach commodity status — with highly capable open-source alternatives emerging weekly — the bottleneck has shifted from intelligence to compute power. You can download a model that rivals GPT-4 for free. Running it continuously? That requires high-end GPUs humming around the clock, consuming electricity and rack space you probably don’t have.

Meanwhile, an absurd inefficiency persists: your gaming PC’s RTX 4090 sits idle 90% of the day. Multiply this across millions of consumer GPUs, and you have massive untapped compute power scattered across bedrooms, home offices, and data centers with excess capacity.

Enter the Agentic Resource Commons

The Agentic Resource Commons (ARC) is a protocol designed for the next phase of AI infrastructure: one where intelligence and compute are fundamentally decoupled, and where the network effect works in reverse — your idle hardware becomes your stake in a global compute cooperative.

Here’s the core insight: AI agents don’t need to live where you work. Your personal agent — the one that knows your preferences, has access to your files, and executes tasks on your behalf — can run anywhere in a trusted network. What matters is sovereignty over the agent itself, not physical custody of the silicon running it.

ARC creates a “Compute Co-op” where:

  • You earn ARC Credits by contributing your hardware when you’re not using it
  • You spend those credits when you need to run your agents 24/7
  • The network dynamically allocates resources based on real-time demand
  • Everyone benefits from pooled infrastructure without surrendering control

Think of it as Airbnb for compute, but instead of renting out spare bedrooms, you’re monetizing your GPU’s downtime.

The Technical Architecture: Smarter Than It Sounds

The magic happens through three interconnected innovations:

1. Disaggregated Inference

Modern AI inference splits naturally into two phases:

Prefill (compute-intensive): Processing your prompt, loading context, generating the first token. This phase benefits from parallel processing and raw computational power.

Decode (latency-sensitive): Generating subsequent tokens one by one, requiring fast memory access and low latency.

Major cloud providers like NVIDIA have already proven this works. Their Dynamo framework and similar systems from Meta and Google demonstrate that splitting these phases across different hardware can deliver 3–4x throughput improvements. ARC applies this same principle to a distributed network: your initial query might hit a high-powered GPU cluster for prefill, then shift to a lower-power node closer to you for decode.

This isn’t theoretical. In 2025, systems like llm-d achieved 2,200 output tokens per second per GPU by intelligently routing prefill and decode tasks to specialized nodes. The technical challenges — transferring KV caches between nodes, maintaining session state — have been solved in production environments.

2. Sovereign Pods

Your agent lives in a “Sovereign Pod” — a portable container you own but don’t necessarily host. It’s cryptographically sealed, meaning:

  • The node executing your agent can’t read your private data
  • You can move the pod between nodes without losing state
  • Execution is verified through trusted computing enclaves (TEEs)

This solves the privacy problem that killed earlier distributed computing attempts. In the early 2000s, projects like SETI@home and Folding@home failed to gain commercial traction partly because organizations couldn’t trust strangers’ computers with sensitive data. TEEs — now standard in enterprise cloud deployments — change this calculus entirely.

3. Proof of Useful Work (PoUW)

Traditional blockchain mining burns electricity solving meaningless mathematical puzzles. ARC flips this: nodes prove they successfully executed your agent’s reasoning task instead.

When your agent needs to analyze a document, write code, or process data, the hosting node:

  1. Performs the computation
  2. Generates a cryptographic proof of execution
  3. Receives ARC Credits only after verification

This creates a consensus mechanism where the “work” is actually useful — it’s your agent getting things done. Recent implementations by projects like Ambient and others have demonstrated that AI computation itself can serve as blockchain consensus, eliminating the wasteful proof-of-work paradox.

The Economics: How the Credit System Works

The Compute Co-op operates on elegant supply-and-demand mechanics:

When you contribute hardware:

  • Your gaming rig earns credits during work hours when you’re away
  • A laptop earns less than a high-end GPU, but it still earns
  • Reliability matters: consistent uptime increases your earning rate

When you consume resources:

  • Off-peak hours (3 AM globally): credits go further
  • Peak hours (9 AM-6 PM in major time zones): compute costs more
  • Complex agents (with high prefill requirements) cost more credits than simple ones

The pricing mechanism is automatic, driven by real-time network utilization. This creates natural incentives for geographic diversity — nodes in Australia benefit from arbitrage during North American work hours, and vice versa.

The escrow mechanism adds accountability: For mission-critical tasks like deploying code or financial transactions, payment is held until automated validation confirms success. Poor performance? The hosting node doesn’t get paid.

Why This Could Actually Work (Unlike Previous Attempts)

Distributed computing has failed before. SETI@home, while noble, never became a commercial platform. Cryptocurrency mining networks prove coordination at scale but produce nothing useful. What’s different now?

1. Demand is Real and Growing

The decentralized GPU market grew from $9 billion in 2024 to projected $100 billion by 2032. Over 50% of generative AI companies cite GPU shortages as their primary scaling obstacle. Unlike 2000s-era grid computing, the market hunger for distributed inference is demonstrable and massive.

2. Technology Has Caught Up

  • TEEs are production-ready: Apple’s Secure Enclave, Intel SGX, and ARM TrustZone enable confidential computing at scale
  • Disaggregated inference works: NVIDIA, Meta, and open-source frameworks have proven the architecture
  • High-speed networking: 5G and fiber connectivity make distributed coordination feasible where it wasn’t a decade ago

3. The Hardware Is Already Out There

NVIDIA shipped 30 million consumer GPUs in 2023. Apple sells 20+ million Macs with M-series chips annually. That’s millions of A100-equivalent compute units entering homes every year. The infrastructure exists; it just needs coordination.

4. Tokenomics Create True Network Effects

Unlike earlier distributed systems that relied on altruism, ARC Credits create genuine economic incentives. The more hardware joins, the more reliable and affordable the network becomes — attracting more users who need compute, which attracts more hardware providers. It’s a flywheel, not a donation drive.

Governance: Beyond “Code is Law”

The Compute Co-op is governed by a DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization), but not the naive “vote on everything” kind that plagued early crypto projects. Instead:

Hardware Standards Committee: Sets minimum requirements for nodes (security, bandwidth, uptime)

Protocol Upgrades: Community votes on feature additions, but with skin-in-the-game weighting — nodes with higher reliability get more voting power

Anti-Free-Rider Mechanisms: You can’t just consume without contributing. Accounts that consistently drain more credits than they earn face rate limiting unless they:

  • Contribute hardware
  • Provide data for model fine-tuning
  • Manually verify agent outputs (human-in-the-loop validation)

This creates a balanced ecosystem where everyone participates, but no one can hijack the network.

Real-World Rollout: Edge Cases and Micro-Nodes

The vision extends beyond enthusiast gaming PCs:

Smart thermostats with ARM chips could handle lightweight inference during HVAC idle time. Gaming consoles already contain capable GPUs that sit unused 16+ hours daily. Laptops in corporate fleets could contribute processing during off-hours, monetizing hardware companies already own.

This “micro-node” strategy dramatically expands the addressable compute pool while distributing load in ways that reduce individual hardware strain.

Privacy remains paramount: All edge devices use hardware-based isolation. Your smart thermostat processing someone else’s agent can’t access your home network, and the inference task itself is opaque — nodes see encrypted computation, not raw data.

Why Centralized Cloud Won’t Just Fix This

You might ask: won’t AWS, Google, and Azure simply build more data centers and drop prices?

Three reasons this is unlikely:

1. Capital Intensity: Building new data centers requires billions in upfront investment and 18–24 month lead times. The AI boom is moving faster than construction schedules.

2. Geographic Constraints: Regulatory requirements increasingly mandate data residency. Decentralized networks naturally provide regional compute without building country-specific data centers.

3. Oligopoly Pricing: As of 2025, four companies control 80% of global cloud compute. This concentration supports premium pricing. Only competitive pressure — like decentralized alternatives — changes this.

We’re already seeing early signs: CoreWeave, originally a crypto mining company, is now IPOing as an AI infrastructure provider. Render Network processed 60+ million frames for creative professionals, proving decentralized GPU pools can handle production workloads.

The Skeptic’s Checklist

“Latency will kill user experience.”

Modern prefill/decode disaggregation already operates across geographic regions with <50ms added latency. For most agent tasks (email drafting, code review, document analysis), sub-second delays are acceptable. Real-time voice? That stays centralized. Everything else can distribute.

“Security is impossible without centralized control.”

This was true in 2005. TEEs, zero-knowledge proofs, and secure enclaves have fundamentally changed the security model. Apple processes Siri requests locally in secure enclaves; Intel SGX enables confidential computing in untrusted environments. The cryptographic primitives exist and are battle-tested.

“Network effects favor incumbents.”

True for social networks. Less true for commodity infrastructure. AWS won by being early, not by being beloved. If a decentralized alternative offers 40–60% cost savings (as early DePIN networks demonstrate), enterprises will adopt it for non-critical workloads first, then migrate incrementally.

“Coordination is too hard.”

Projects like io.net, GPU.NET, and Aethir have already coordinated thousands of GPUs across continents with >99% uptime. The coordination problem is solved; ARC applies those lessons to agent-specific workflows.

The Post-Subscription Future

In five years, paying a monthly SaaS fee for AI capabilities will seem as antiquated as paying per-minute for phone calls. The intelligence will be abundant and free. What you’ll actually pay for — and what the ARC Protocol enables — is on-demand access to distributed compute resources.

Your agent will live in the cloud, but not Amazon’s cloud or Google’s. It will live in a decentralized cooperative where millions of participants contribute resources and share benefits.

The laptop you’re reading this on? It could be earning you credits right now while you scroll.

Your gaming PC gathering dust during work hours? It could be subsidizing your agent’s compute costs.

That’s not a distant vision. The technology exists. The demand is real. The infrastructure is being built as you read this.

The question isn’t whether this future arrives. It’s whether legacy cloud providers adapt fast enough to compete with it — or whether, like so many technological transitions before, the incumbents watch from the sidelines as a more efficient model takes hold.
