Why Understanding How Agents Think Makes You a Better Product Manager

· Originally published on Notion ↗

The Realization

I've spent years designing search interfaces for e-commerce. Filters. Facets. Category trees. I watched users click through 12 tabs, compare specs across three sites, and still leave unsure if the lens they wanted was compatible with their camera body.

I knew the problem. We all knew. But the tools we had—static pages, predefined filters, rigid category hierarchies—couldn't solve it. We built interfaces for databases because that's what technology allowed. Users were trying to solve problems: "I want to start a YouTube channel. What gear do I need?" Yet our interfaces forced them to become camera experts first.

AI agents change what's possible. This is the problem AI agents promise to solve. But here's what most product discussions miss: “The interface isn't the product. The reasoning is”

I built ShopSense AI—a proof-of-concept agent for consumer electronics—to understand this deeply. Not to ship a feature, but to understand how agents think about helping people find the right gear. Github: link

ShopSense AI is a ReAct agent for consumer electronics discovery, built on the SAP Spartacus electronics sample catalog (via API Mock):

  • 16 products: Cameras (Canon, Nikon, Fujifilm, Sony), video equipment, accessories, films
  • 4 categories: Cameras, Video, Accessories, Films & Media
  • 6 solution bundles: Pre-configured kits for common use cases
  • Editorial design: Browser Company–inspired warm cream UI with generative right panel

The brand positioning: "Find your perfect camera, intelligently matched."

This isn't B2B procurement. This is a consumer navigating a complex purchase decision with constraints they may not fully understand yet.

One Core Insight: Intent Beats Interface

Traditional product thinking optimizes the path from A to B. Reduce clicks. Streamline flows. AI agents invert this:

ShopSense has two parts:

  1. A ReAct reasoning engine that thinks through what the user actually needs
  2. A generative interface that reshapes itself based on detected intent

Most demos focus on the second part. They show chatbots with product carousels. What they miss is the first part—the reasoning—and that's where the product value actually lives.

How the Agent Thinks: The ReAct Pattern

The ReAct pattern (Reasoning + Acting) comes from Yao et al. (2022). At its core:

Thought → Action → Observation → ... → Final Answer

Here's what a real reasoning trace looks like for ShopSense:

User: "I need a camera for wildlife photography under $1000"

Agent's internal monologue:

Thought: This is a product recommendation request with specific constraints:

  • Use case: wildlife photography (needs telephoto capability, fast shutter)
  • Budget: under $1000
  • Category: cameras

Action: search_products(query="wildlife photography camera", category="cameras")

Observation: Found 4 cameras. Canon EOS 450D ($449.99) has 12.2MP,

but wildlife typically needs longer lenses. Sony HDR-FX7E ($999.99)

is video-focused, not ideal. Nikon D40 ($319.99) is entry-level.

Thought: None of these cameras alone are ideal for wildlife photography

under $1000—they need telephoto lenses. I should check if there are

compatible lenses in the accessories category and calculate total cost.

Action: search_products(query="telephoto lens zoom", category="accessories")

Observation: Found Canon EF 50mm lens ($199.99)—not telephoto enough

for wildlife. Need to check if there are longer zoom lenses.

Thought: The catalog may not have dedicated wildlife gear. I should

recommend the best available option with an honest caveat about

lens requirements for wildlife photography.

Final Answer: [Recommendation with transparent limitations]

Notice what's happening:

  • The agent classifies intent (recommendation with constraints)
  • It extracts constraints (budget, use case) as structured data
  • It sequences actions based on dependencies
  • It interprets observations honestly—even when the catalog has gaps

This isn't prompt engineering. This is reasoning about product fit.

Solution Building: The Real Innovation

Traditional e-commerce sells products. ShopSense explores selling solutions.

When a user says: "I'm setting up a YouTube studio"—they don't want a camera. They want:

  • A camera (with flip screen for monitoring)
  • Lighting (adjustable color temperature)
  • Audio (USB connectivity for easy computer integration)
  • All compatible, all within budget

ShopSense's solution cart lets users:

  1. Search across categories with a unified goal
  2. See running totals
  3. Check compatibility (USB vs XLR, mount types, etc.)
  4. Save configurations

This is the difference between selling products and solving problems.

The Generative UI: Adapting to Intent

The right panel of ShopSense changes based on intent:

  • grid for browsing
  • compare for side-by-side analysis
  • bundle for solution configuration

Example evolution:

User: "Build me a studio kit for under $5000"

Right Panel Updates:

  1. First: Hero section with headline "Building Your Studio Setup"
  2. Then: Camera options with "Solo Creator" badges (Canon R50, Sony ZV-E1)
  3. Then: Compatible lighting kits (Neewer LED panels)
  4. Then: USB microphones (Rode NT-USB+)
  5. Finally: Complete bundle with total, compatibility check, and savings

The UI evolves as the agent gathers information. This isn't a chatbot with carousels. This is an interface that understands what you're building.

Why This Matters for Product Strategy

1. Problem-Solving Over Product-Pushing

Most e-commerce optimizes for conversion. ShopSense optimizes for confidence.

When the agent explains why it recommended the Canon R50 for YouTube (flip screen, 4K, under $1000), the user understands the fit. They're not just buying—they're learning.

2. Constraint Handling Builds Trust

In ShopSense, constraints are first-class objects:

@dataclass

class Constraint:

type: ConstraintType # BUDGET, SPACE, EXPERIENCE_LEVEL

value: Any

source: ConstraintSource # EXPLICIT, INFERRED

When the agent says: "The Sony A7IV is excellent but exceeds your $2000 budget. Here are alternatives..."—this is explainability, not just filtering.

3. Transparency in Limitations

The agent has 16 products. It can't solve every use case. When a user asks for wildlife photography gear and the catalog lacks telephoto lenses, the agent should say so:

"I found cameras in your budget, but dedicated wildlife photography typically requires 200mm+ telephoto lenses which aren't in our current catalog. Would you like recommendations for general photography instead, or suggestions for where to find compatible lenses?"

This honesty builds more trust than pretending the catalog has everything.

What I Got Wrong (And Fixed)

First attempt: I exposed the raw reasoning trace.

Users saw every thought, action, and observation. Transparent, but overwhelming. Product lesson: Transparency doesn't mean exposing internals.

Second attempt: I showed only the final answer.

Clean, but users didn't trust recommendations. They couldn't see why the agent suggested these products. Product lesson: Trust requires visibility into reasoning, not raw logs.

Final approach: Summarized reasoning with expandable details.

"I searched cameras for YouTube studios (4 found), checked compatibility with USB audio (5 mics found), and assembled options within your budget."

Expand for full trace. This is the balance: signal, not noise.

The Limitations (This Is a Proof of Concept)

ShopSense answers: Can an agent reason about consumer electronics purchases? Yes. The next question—Can this scale to production?—requires solving different problems.

The Product Manager's Dilemma

Here's the tension:

  • You don't need to be an engineer to build with AI. But you do need to understand how it fails.

I built this to understand:

  • Where does reasoning break down?
  • How do constraints propagate?
  • What does "explainability" look like to users?
  • Where's the line between helpful guidance and annoying hand-holding?

These are product questions. They require technical fluency but not technical execution.

The Strategic Takeaway

If you're a PM evaluating AI for commerce, here's my framework:

  1. Map the problem, not the interface

Before designing UI flows, understand how an expert would help someone shop. What questions do they ask? What trade-offs do they explain?

  1. Constraints are features, not filters

Budget. Use case. Experience level. These aren't search filters—they're inputs to reasoning. Design your agent to understand them structurally.

  1. Intent classification is the foundation

If you don't know what the user is trying to accomplish, you can't generate the right experience. Invest here before investing in chat interfaces.

  1. Sell solutions, not products

The opportunity isn't "find a camera." It's "help me start YouTube." The agent that understands the goal wins over the agent that searches the catalog.

  1. Transparency builds trust, but logs don't

Users want to know why the agent made a recommendation. They don't want to see the raw reasoning trace. Find the middle ground.

Conclusion

ShopSense AI taught me that AI agents in commerce aren't about replacing product pages with chat. They're about understanding what the user is trying to accomplish and generating the right experience for that goal.

The generative UI is cool. But it's a symptom of a deeper pattern: the agent reasons about intent, constraints, and compatibility before anything renders on screen.

For product managers, the implication is clear: You don't need to build the agent yourself. But you need to understand how it thinks well enough to design products around its capabilities and limitations.

That's the work.
